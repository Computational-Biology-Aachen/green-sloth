The [Ebenhöh 2014](https://doi.org/10.1098/rstb.2013.0223) model is a kinetic model of the photosynthetic electron transport chain (PETC), built to study how a plant acclimates to changing light on short time scales. It resolves the core electron-transfer machinery — photosystem II (PSII), the plastoquinone (PQ) pool, the cytochrome b6f complex, plastocyanin, photosystem I (PSI), ferredoxin, FNR and ATP synthase — as a compact system of ordinary differential equations, tracking the redox states of the mobile carriers together with the stromal ATP and NADPH pools and the lumen proton concentration. On top of the linear chain it includes the alternative electron routes (cyclic electron flow, PTOX and the NDH-mediated reduction of PQ) and the regulatory processes that respond to light, namely the proton-motive-force-driven qE quenching and the state transitions that redistribute the light-harvesting complexes (LHCs) between the two photosystems through reversible phosphorylation.

The model was developed to reproduce, from first principles, the basic chlorophyll-fluorescence behaviour observed during short-term light acclimation, in particular the dynamics of state transitions, and to provide a mechanistic, literature-derived parameterisation of the electron transport chain rather than a fit to a single dataset. It is one of the foundational kinetic descriptions of the PETC and a direct ancestor of the later [Matuszynska 2016](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bbabio.2016.09.003) and [Matuszynska 2019](https://doi.org/10.1111/ppl.12962) models, which reuse and extend much of its electron-transport structure. For that reason it is included in GreenSloth as a clear, well-documented starting point for understanding how the light reactions of photosynthesis are commonly modelled.
